‘can history be written out of tweets?’ is a falsely naïve question.

  • of course, tweets can be and will be primary sources.
    • in history or rather mnemohistory or memory studies, it is already a primary source. I’ll give some examples from my own research - that will be my first part.

In fact, just an illustration that since the School of the Annales (at least), the answer to the question ‘what is a primary source’ is wider and wider. All along the 20th and 21st centuries, historians have conquered more and more fields by considering new kinds of primary sources. A good example of that is the rise of oral history.

  • volume

access

from dependence to apicalypse

The problem of this research is that it was depdendant on the willingness of Twitter to allow us access to its data. And for a long time, Twitter has been a researcher’s eldorado.

Twitter allowed access via its API – a way, here, to connect to Twitter to get its data in a quite structured form (135 metadata / json). For free: first via its streaming set of commands, and then, for a couple of years, via a specific product for researchers, still free, after a small procedure to get recognized as researcher by twitter (through the new APIv2).

Both kinds of access had their methodological limitations, but it made collecting data so easy.

With deborah paci, for instance, we could collect data on the commemoration of the end of the second world war in italy and france during the lockdowns in 2020. It was easy, fast, the data was well structured, etc.

But that is over. For two reasons:

  • Elon Musk
  • Large Language Models

Why LLM? Data is all the more valuable (financially speaking) today that the numerous large language models that are available or being developped incited many platforms to close their access to data (twitter and reddit in 2023 for instance). And they closed it fully: they closed their apis (or made it much more expensive) and limited the ways we had before to scrap data online.

this is the consequence of our dependance, in the age of big data, towards the big web platforms. and this might prevents us to access data and just do our work.

digital dark age

this dependance is all the more important that libraries and archive centres do what they can but cannot do much.

From 2006 to 2018, twitter is archived at the Library of Congress. But today, the LoC is unable to give access to this archive. Among the resasons of this archive being a ‘dark archive’, to use Lise Jaillant’s expression, it has not the technology to let people search it. It would take days to answer a query, if not weeks or months.

But Twitter is here an exception: facebook, most google products, etc., are just not archived or rather are self-archived. What would happen if those societies disappear? their data will probably be lost.

and in a way, lots of data, in the 1990s, have been lost. So why not more recent data?

volume

big data

what we speak about is big data. the #ww1 is an easy project: 9 millions tweets is manageable, all the more that for most analyses, retweets should be removed.

it’s a different story with #covid19. This project, originally, was using the same ways to collect data, mostly tweets in french: 65 millions tweets in 4 years. and there are tweets dataset on covid that are much larger.

hard to exploit 65 millions tweets without strongly thinking about methods.

methods

you don’t use the same methods / here I mean methods as algorithms / with 9 million and 65 million tweets, at last without sampling. But in our case sampling resulted in very different results, even while keeping the same sampling method. The data was, in the end, too diverse, event if made out of tweets.

the tools I have used for the #ww1 project could be used with the work we did with deborah’s because we limited the number of tweets we analysed.

but if I don’t want to limit it? then I need to switch method. and an unavoidable family of method will be ai.

ai

we use too much the acronym “ai”. Here, I’m speaking about machine and deep learning, which is already something wide.

and there are different ways to use that kinds of ai. MALLET for instance, for Latent Dircihlet Allocation, the dominant way to perform topic modelling, is already based on machine learning, with training, etc. But it’s a first step to analyse large dataset.

with more advanced ai-based systems, including some uses of large language models, problem is: infrastructure. Of course we can use those systems for our research, but we need a performant digital infrastructure.

and here, we go from access / volume to inequalities. All universities, all the more when those specialising in humanities and social sciences, are / will not be able to acquire and maintain such infrastructures.
